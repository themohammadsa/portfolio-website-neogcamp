A lion is in front of you right now. What do you do?

Posted on LinkedIn

June 22, 2020


It's obvious, you run. When you run, you are going to need a lot of energy to the muscles of your body.

1. This happens by a hormone called cortisol, this helps in transporting glucose/energy to the muscles --- meaning your sugar level rises.

2. This glucose is transported through blood and this needs to be rushed to your muscles real fast. This leads to increasing the pressure of your blood through your blood vessels --- your BP rises.

3. The blood pressure is increased by 'constricting/shrinking' your arteries so that the blood travels faster. Our blood vessels at times have fats and dirt deposited in them, and when this shrinking occurs, the arteries can get blocked -- leading to heart attacks.


Do you think you are going to feel hungry or any other emotion when there is a lion in front of you?

No, right? This is because there are two types of nervous systems in the body. They are the Parasympathetic (System A) and Sympathetic Nervous System (System B).

1. System A works when you are at rest, digestion process takes place, excretion process, body healing, etc.,

2. System B becomes active when you're facing a threat/emergency. (Releasing of cortisol and adrenaline)

(Note: Both the systems (A&B) work autonomously)

ONLY one system works at a time. And that is why you are not going to feel any other emotion when you are in a threat situation.

Now replace the lion with stress/tension. That's the same flow of hormones which occur inside the body during both the situations.


The problem doesn't stop here, it aggravates further.

Excess cortisol causes excess sugar to become fat deposits in unwanted places in the body (leading to obesity), can even lead to over-eating (due to excess energy in the body tricking your mind into thinking you are hungry when you are not), can affect your circadian rhythm (sleep-wake-cycle), destroy the general adaptive routine of the body.



Note: This is just a brief explanation of the processes involved in the human body during stress/tension as simple as possible, might have left some medical terms out!
